Progress in Reducing Child Deaths

The decline in child mortality (deaths of children under age 5, including infants and newborns) over the past two decades has been described as one of the greatest success stories in global health.

*Child death rate represents the number of deaths per 1,000 live births for a given year

The tremendous success in reducing child death rates led Bill Gates to name this chart "the most beautiful chart in the world."

However, Projections from the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) show that progress toward reducing child mortality is expected to slow in the coming years.

The current trajectory fails to meet the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goal target (SDG target) for all countries to reduce child deaths to no more than 25 per 1,000 live births by 2030.

Meeting the SDG target by 2030 may require additional health system strengthening, as the current trajectory does not put the world on track to meet global goals for reducing child deaths.

How can we close the gap to save millions of children's lives?

National Disparities in Child Death Rates

Child death rate

Good data are critical for tracking progress toward reducing child deaths.

By mapping where child deaths occur, down to a fine geographic scale, we can better pinpoint where to focus efforts to save child lives.

Newly published research from IHME reveals persistent disparities in where child deaths occur.

In 2017, 93% of child deaths occurred in the low- and middle-income countries shown here, where about 60% of the world’s population lives.

In the following views, neutral colors represent areas that have met the SDG target.

Cooler colors represent areas that have exceeded the SDG target.

Warmer colors represent areas that haven't met the SDG target.

Peru is an example of progress.

Between 2000 and 2017, Peru dramatically reduced child death rates.

See the the Local Burden of Disease visualization to explore Peru in more detail

At the national level, Peru’s child death rate in 2000 was 38.6 deaths per 1,000 live births.

This animation shows how Peru gradually reduced their child death rate to 15.5 deaths per 1,000 live births by 2017.

At the level of individual provinces, Peru has also kept inequality relatively low, although the child death rate still varies.

Countries and regions that have shown progress in reducing child deaths can offer valuable insights into effective interventions and serve as models for success elsewhere.

Looking at Nigeria and Ethiopia helps add context to how the distribution of child death rates has changed over time.

See the the Local Burden of Disease visualization to explore Nigeria and Ethiopia in more detail.

Both Nigeria (left) and Ethiopia (right) had high child death rates in 2000.

Let's compare the distributions of child death rates across Nigeria, Ethiopia, and Peru to see how those rates have changed from 2000 to 2017.

Geographic inequality in child death rates is represented by the vertical "spread" from the top to the bottom circles and the space in between adjacent circles.

Nigeria and Ethiopia had high inequality in child death rates in 2000.

Between 2000 and 2017, all three countries lowered their child death rates, but each faced different challenges.

By 2017, Nigeria made progress, but still had some of the highest child death rates among low- and middle-income countries.

Ethiopia reduced its child death rates by more than half and significantly reduced inequality.

Peru brought almost all provinces down to meet or exceed the SDG target.

The best performing areas with the lowest child death rate were Edo for Nigeria, Addis Ababa for Ethiopia, and Lima for Peru.

Imagine a scenario with no inequality, where every province, district, or zone within a country lowered their child death rates to match the lowest rate within their country in 2017.

Scaling the circles by the number of children who would live past age 5 illustrates how many children could be saved by reducing inequality in each country.

In this scenario, Nigeria could have saved 348,818 children in 2017, Ethiopia could have saved 96,990 children, and Peru could have saved 4,946 children.

If all of the countries studied made the same gains to eliminate inequality, 2.7 million child deaths could have been averted in 2017, moving us closer to the global goal of ending preventable child deaths by 2030.

These detailed estimates reveal trends and patterns that can help us better understand where to focus efforts to prevent child deaths.

Every region has different challenges, and by knowing what the obstacles are at a local level, decision-makers can better strategize how to overcome them.

Reaching the SDG target means more than just achieving a number.

Success means that millions of children will have the chance to survive and thrive.
